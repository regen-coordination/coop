# Coop Strategy

This document is about sequencing, tradeoffs, and execution discipline. It is not the vision doc
and not the monetization doc.

## 1. Strategic Thesis

Coop wins if it becomes the most trustworthy path from fragmented context to shared action and
capital formation for small and medium-sized communities.

That means we are not trying to win by:

- being the loudest AI product
- being the most autonomous agent product
- being the most onchain-maximal product
- being a generic productivity suite

We are trying to win by being the clearest and safest coordination membrane from knowledge into
opportunity.

## 2. Four Core Tenets

These are not branding lines. They are strategic constraints.

### 1. Browser-First

Why it matters:

- the browser is where knowledge fragmentation already happens
- capture quality is highest where context is first encountered
- adoption is easier when the product meets the user inside existing work

Strategic implication:

- extension quality matters more than broadening into too many shells too early

### 2. Local-First

Why it matters:

- trust collapses when users cannot tell what stayed private and what leaked
- many community contexts are too sensitive for default-cloud coordination
- local-first makes walkaway, portability, and forkability more credible

Strategic implication:

- treat local processing and explicit sharing boundaries as a core product differentiator, not a
  secondary implementation detail

### 3. Passkey-First

Why it matters:

- wallet-extension-first onboarding narrows the audience too early
- passkeys give Coop a cleaner path from mainstream usability to onchain capability

Strategic implication:

- identity and signing flows should feel simple first, powerful second

### 4. Explicit Publish

Why it matters:

- communities need a human judgment boundary
- generated output without review undermines trust in shared memory
- this is the line between assistive coordination and silent automation

Strategic implication:

- protect review quality even when that slows down product expansion

## 3. What We Build First

The product should deepen in this order:

### First: reliable capture and review

If capture is noisy or review is unclear, every later layer becomes brittle.

### Second: legible publish and archive

If groups cannot trust what became shared and what became durable, the memory layer fails.

### Third: bounded action rails

Only after shared memory is credible should action rails become central to the story.

### Fourth: extensible coordination primitives

Only after the working loop feels trustworthy should Coop OS become more than an internal
architecture frame.

## 4. Strategic Bets

### Bet 1: Trust beats automation theater

Many products will promise more autonomy faster. Coop should resist that pressure. Communities that
care about durable memory, funding, governance, and impact work need trustworthy structure more
than they need maximal automation.

### Bet 2: Knowledge into opportunity is the wedge

The product gets materially stronger when capture and synthesis do not end at insight. They should
help a community move toward:

- funding leads
- capital formation briefs
- partnership opportunities
- clearer next-step proposals

If Coop becomes the place where a group's most useful context matures, then archive, delegation,
proof, and action rails become natural extensions rather than forced add-ons.

### Bet 3: Shared memory makes capital formation legible

If Coop becomes the place where a group's strongest evidence matures, then grants, funding
decisions, public-goods staking, and other capital-allocation workflows can point to a clearer
shared record instead of private fragments and informal memory.

### Bet 4: Open, local, and verifiable is a real market position

There is a meaningful group of communities, builders, and funders that want an alternative to
cloud-dependent coordination systems with unclear ownership boundaries.

### Bet 5: The first wedge is communities with real coordination pressure

The strongest early users are not passive consumers. They are groups already doing:

- grant coordination
- capital allocation
- ecosystem research
- governance work
- regenerative or place-based collaboration
- mission-driven project operations

## 5. Durgadas Standards As Strategic Constraints

We are using the Durgadas
[Coordination Structural Integrity Suite](https://github.com/durgadasji/standards)
as an external guide for how the strategy should behave under pressure.

### Precision-First Design Standard

Strategic rule:

- say exactly what is live, what is mock, and what is future

This protects the product from inflated claims and protects the team from building strategy on
self-flattery.

### Adverse-Signal Engagement Principle Core Standard

Strategic rule:

- product criticism, failed demos, unclear onboarding, and user confusion are not noise

They are part of the design input.

### Structural Consent Legibility Standard

Strategic rule:

- users must be able to tell what stays local, what becomes shared, and what authorizes action

If this line blurs, the trust thesis weakens.

### Information Asymmetry Classification Standard

Strategic rule:

- hidden dependencies and hidden authority need to be surfaced early

That includes:

- who can publish
- who can approve
- who can archive
- who can act onchain
- who understands the system well enough to challenge it

### Structural Power Distribution Standard

Strategic rule:

- do not let convenience quietly centralize control

The product should make roles, escalation, and bounded authority more legible over time, not less.

## 6. What We Deliberately Avoid For Now

- generic autonomous execution as a default experience
- token-first go-to-market
- broad social feed dynamics
- enterprise surface-area expansion before the core loop is trusted
- adding more product surfaces faster than we improve the main extension and receiver experience

## 7. Success Criteria

Near-term strategy is working if:

- the demo story is legible without heavy explanation
- the product can show how captured knowledge becomes opportunity
- first-run trust boundaries are easy to explain
- early communities can use the product without custom hand-holding every time
- archive and action rails strengthen the memory story instead of confusing it
- capital-formation and funding use cases feel native instead of bolted on
- the team can describe current posture honestly without weakening the long-term case
