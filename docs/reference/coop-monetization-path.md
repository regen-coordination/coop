# Coop Monetization Path

This document separates the value-capture story from the product vision and product strategy.

The framing here is deliberately disciplined:

- the core loop stays open and legible
- monetization should align with real coordination value
- value capture should not depend on obscuring power, fees, or lock-in

For current grant targets, see [Grant Landscape](/reference/grant-landscape-2026-03).

## 1. Monetization Principle

Coop should monetize the coordination infrastructure that creates ongoing leverage or ongoing cost,
not the basic right to capture, refine, and review knowledge locally.

That means the base loop should remain accessible:

- capture
- local synthesis
- review
- publication into a coop

Paid value should show up where the system adds durable operational leverage:

- archive permanence
- hosted coordination infrastructure
- specialized operator tooling
- institutional deployment
- premium automation and integration layers

## 2. Durgadas Constraint On Monetization

The Durgadas standards matter here because monetization can become a covert governance structure.

We should use the framework to avoid that.

### Precision

Fees, limits, and paid tiers must be legible. No hidden gating and no vague pricing promises that
mask the actual tradeoff.

### Consent

Users and coops should understand what they are paying for and what new authority or dependency
comes with that payment.

### Information asymmetry

Do not build the business around advantages users cannot see, such as unclear archive dependencies,
unclear custody, or unclear operator powers.

### Power distribution

Revenue should not quietly centralize the coordination system into a vendor-controlled black box.

## 3. Phase 1: Grants And Contracts

This is the correct near-term path.

### Why first

- the product is still being hardened
- ecosystem-aligned R&D is a genuine part of the work
- communities may need guided deployments before self-serve pricing makes sense

### What fits here

- ecosystem grants
- research and development grants
- pilot deployment contracts
- implementation partnerships
- integration work with aligned protocols or community infrastructure

### What is being sold

- product hardening
- tailored setup and deployment
- integration and protocol support
- applied research on coordination tooling

### Risks

- becoming a services shop with no product focus
- bending the roadmap around one-off sponsor needs

### Guardrail

- use grants and contracts to finance product maturity, not to permanently replace a product model

## 4. Phase 2: Protocol Fees

Protocol fees should come from real infrastructure value, not speculative abstraction.

### Best candidates

- durable archive and retrieval beyond a free tier
- hosted sync or coop infrastructure for larger groups
- advanced operator tooling
- premium workflow automation
- premium connector or knowledge graph services

### Why this is aligned

- the fees track real cost or real leverage
- the basic local-first workflow remains intact without payment

### Preconditions

- reliable product loop
- clear usage metrics
- clear free versus paid boundary
- legible archive and hosting guarantees

## 5. Phase 3: Licensing

Licensing becomes appropriate when Coop OS starts to function as deployable coordination
infrastructure rather than only one product.

### What licensing can mean

- institutional deployment packages
- specialized operator or admin tooling
- verticalized distributions
- branded or embedded versions of Coop OS

### What it should not mean

- closing the base protocol
- making fundamental interoperability proprietary
- charging for the basic right to use the commons-shaped core

### Strategic use

Licensing is a way to capture value from organizations that need support, certainty, packaging, or
deeper operational features without locking smaller communities out of the base loop.

## 6. Phase 4: Tokenization

Tokenization should be treated as a late coordination design question, not the opening business
model.

### When it becomes worth discussing

- after durable product usage exists
- after fee flows are legible
- after governance and accountability mechanisms are mature enough to hold shared ownership

### Legitimate reasons to consider it

- aligning long-term stewardship
- distributing protocol-level fee upside
- coordinating treasury and ecosystem growth
- rewarding meaningful network contribution

### Illegitimate reasons to consider it

- replacing product traction with speculative attention
- using token design to paper over unclear monetization
- introducing governance complexity before the core system is stable

### Required guardrails

- explicit fee and power disclosure
- bounded governance powers
- credible anti-capture design
- no claim that tokenization equals decentralization

## 7. What Stays Free

The cleanest long-term posture is:

- local capture stays free
- local synthesis stays free
- review stays free
- basic cooperative publishing stays free

That protects the legitimacy of the project and supports the strongest grant, public-goods, and
community-adoption narratives.

## 8. Recommended Sequence

### Stage A

- grants and contracts finance the hardening phase

### Stage B

- protocol fees emerge where archive, hosting, or operator features create real recurring value

### Stage C

- licensing expands Coop OS into institutional and vertical use cases

### Stage D

- tokenization is evaluated only after real usage, legible governance, and real fee flows exist

## 9. Anti-Patterns To Avoid

- inventing SaaS tiers before the product posture is real
- leading the story with token design
- charging for the base trust posture that makes Coop valuable in the first place
- hiding operational dependency inside vague "infrastructure" language
- allowing the business model to undermine local-first legitimacy

## 10. Practical Read

The near-term story should be:

> Coop is open, early, and product-first. Near-term funding comes from grants and aligned contracts.
> Long-term monetization comes from durable coordination infrastructure, not from gating the basic
> community workflow.
